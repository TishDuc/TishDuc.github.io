Displacements around the globe from 1962-2025

The United Nations Refugee Agency (UNHCR) is the largest global organization providing assistance and protection to forcibly displaced and stateless people. It was founded in 1951 in the aftermath of the second World War, and was originally only intended to provide temporary assistance to European refugees. However, over the years, the UNHCR's mandate has expanded to include people of all nationalities and in all regions of the world. Today, the UNHCR provides assistance to millions of refugees, asylum seekers, internally displaced people, and stateless individuals around the world. The UNHCR's work includes providing emergency shelter, food, water, and medical care to displaced people, as well as advocating for their rights and working to find durable solutions to their displacement.

The UNHCR publishes their data about forcibly displaced people and updates it every six months. We are going to dive into one of their datasets: the UNHCR Forced Displacement Flow Dataset, which tracks international displacements around the world, from 1962 to 2025.

In this study we'll specifically look at where refugees and Asylum seekers go, and how far they travel. In other words, when forced to flee their country, what are the distances traveled to reach safety? We will start with a general analysis to give the reader context, and then study displacement patterns of three crisis: Afhganistan (1979-1989), Rwanda (1994-1996), and Ukraine (2022-2025). We'll look at the average distances traveled for each and see if, and how, ther are correlated to flows. Those three conflicts affect different decades and regions of the world, so we aim to see if there are any differences in displacement patterns across time and space.

Let's have a look at our dataset. It consists of over 100 thousand entries and each of those is a 'flow', with an origin country, a destination country, and a number of people registered.

PROS

  • Covers a long time period (1962-2025), 64 years
  • Covers 216 countries
  • Reliable source (UNHCR)
  • Regularly updated
  • The data is largely complete and clean

LIMITATIONS

  • Only tracks international flows
  • Few datapoints the first few years, which limits pattern observation
  • Some entries have "unknown" or "stateless" origin
  • We can only study officially recorded displacements
  • Flows are aggregated year-by-year

The dataset tracks four kinds of status: refugees, asylum claimants, and people in refugee-like situations (IOP, ROC). Notably, it does not track internally displaced people, and this is not an insignificant category. According to the UNHCR 2024 Global Trends Report, internally displaced people accounted for 73.5 million out of the total 123.2 million forcibly displaced people worldwide, approximately 60%, and the percentage has been even higher in previous years. As part of the limitations of our chosen dataset, we will only study international displacements.

Case studies

In this next section, we'll study three of these episodes in more detail, and have a look at where refugees and asylum seekers flee to. Let's study distances traveled and see if there are any differences in the patterns of displacement across time.

AFGHANISTAN

The Soviet-Afghan war took place in Afghanistan from December 1979 to February 1989. Though the conflict officially ends in 1989, the instability persisted long afterward and continued after the U.S. invasion in 2001. These decades of sustained instability produced one of the world's largest and most prolonged refugee crises.

source: UNHCR - https://www.unhcr.org/where-we-work/countries/afghanistan

While the conflict lasted from 1979 to 1989, we detected that the period with the most forced displacement was 1980–1988. Looking at the map, three things stand out immediately:

  1. The sheer number of people fleeing the country, with some years seeing over 2 million people displaced.
  2. The nearby countries of Iran and Pakistan standing out in dark red, accepting the vast majority of Afghan refugees.
  3. A few other countries took in the other refugees, but they are in very small amounts.

"In fact, Afghanistan has experienced one of the world's largest refugee crises for more than two decades. Between the Soviet invasion of Afghanistan in 1979 and the present day, one in four Afghans has been a refugee. At the peak of the crisis in the late 1980s, there were more than six million Afghan refugees."

source: The Forced Migration Review

The sankey diagram to the left confirms just how much of the refugee flow from Afghanistan was concentrated in just two countries, Pakistan and Iran. Pakistan took the most at almost 3.2 million, followed by Iran at nearly 2.5 million, and the ext follow up is Germany, in third with only 21.743. Denmark at took in 272 people. The distances in this Sankey plot (and the Bubble plot that follows) are calculated from the geographic center of Afghanistan to the border of the recipient country. In this chart, we begin to see the relationship between distance and refugee volume. The two countries that received the largest number of refugees are also the geographically closest to Afghanistan, with Pakistan's border located 319 km away, and Iran's 405 km away.

How far do people go?

Let's have a closer look at the average distance traveled by refugees to flee this conflict. An important disclaimer in computing distances traveled is that we don't know exactly how far each refugee actually traveled. We can only compute rough approximation, and it doesn't take into account the fact that refugees may have traveled to different parts of the destination country, or that they may have taken indirect routes to get there. However,it still gives us a general idea of the relationship between distance and number of refugees.

We see the bubbles clustered on the left-hand side of the chart, which shows again most recipient countries were geographically nearby. Again, we see Pakistan and Iran completely dominate. A few European countries also appear, but the numbers are small compared to the whopping 5.7 million that the neighbouring countries received.

Our weighted calculations estimate that Afghan refugees in the period 1980-1988 traveled an average 383 kilometers to reach safety.

RWANDA

The Rwandan genocide, also known as the Tutsi genocide, occurred from 7 April to 19 July 1994 during the Rwandan Civil War. Over a span of around 100 days, members of the Tutsi ethnic group, as well as some moderate Hutu and Twa, were systematically killed by Hutu militias. An estimated 800,000 Rwandans were killed.

source: BBC — Rwanda genocide: 100 days of slaughter

Note that Rwanda's population only numbered 7 million people in 1994.

source: US Holocaust Memorial Museum — Divided by Ethnicity

The genocide was marked by extreme violence, with victims often murdered by neighbours, and widespread sexual violence. It ended when the Tutsi-led Rwandan Patriotic Front (RPF) won the civil war, but refugees continued to leave the country in the following years. The genocide was followed by the First Congo War in 1996, a conflict between the new RPF government and the Democratic Republic of the Congo.

The map shows a massive exodus of more than 2 million people in 1994, the year the genocide occurred. 1995 was a relatively quiet year, although we still see more than 34,000 people depart to Burundi. Then, when the First Congo War breaks out in 1996, we see another 85,000 people leaving. We invite the reader to explore the map and hover over the different flows to see the exact numbers.

Looking at this graph, we notice that Rwandan refugees overwhelmingly fled to neighbouring countries. Over the studies period of time 1.4 million Rwandans fled to the Democratic Republic of the Congo, 700,000 fled to Tanzania, and 294,000 fled to Burundi. We note that Rwanda is only 70km away from the Tanzanian border, only 84km away from DRCongo, and only 44km away from Burundi A few other countries make the list, most of which are African countries (Uganda, South Africa, Malawi, and the Central African Republic), some located ober 800 km awar from Rwanda. Of the European countries, Belgium was the biggest recipient, being over 6.000 km away they oinly took in 1,369 Rwandans.

UKRAINE

In February 2022, Russia launched a full-scale invasion of Ukraine, leading to the bloodiest conflict in Europe since World War II, and a humanitarian crisis which caused the third-biggest wave of refugees in modern history.

source: Council on Foreign Relations — Ukraine: Conflict at the Crossroads of Europe and Russia

The conflict has resulted in widespread destruction, loss of life, and millions forced to flee. Of the three case studies, this is by far the one that got the most media coverage, and we imagine that most readers of this article have some degree of familiarity with it. The conflict is ongoing, so we don't have the whole story yet, but we can already see some interesting differences in the patterns of displacement compared to the previous two case studies.

Immediately, we see a completely different picture for the Ukraine war than for the previous two conflicts. The map shows that Ukrainians fled to a broad range of countries, including some quite far away. For example, Canada received 75,157 Ukrainian refugees in the first year of the conflict, and Spain received 156,923. Displacements peak in 2022 where millions of Ukrainians flee, but they continue to be high in the following years.

The sankey diagram confirms the differences. For the first time, we see a country that is not a direct neighbour of the conflict (Germany) at number one, receiving 1.4 million Ukrainians. The immediate neighbours of Poland and Russia do receive 1.4 and 1.3 million refugees respectively, but are then followed by Czechia at 640,000, another country that does not border Ukraine. The rest of the list is a mix of European countries, some close (Moldova, Romania, Bulgaria) and some further away (the UK, Spain, Italy).

The bubble chart shows a much more even distribution of recipient countries across the distance spectrum. The average distance traveled is more than three times the average distance traveled by Afghan refugees in the 1980s, and more than 14 times the average distance traveled by Rwandan refugees in the 1990s.

Discussion

After taking a closer look at our case studies, we can see a clear pattern emerging. Not too surprisingly, people seem to "favor" proximity in times of crisis. In all three cases the immediate neighbouring countries received the vast majority of displaced people. On average, the further a country is from the observed conflict, the fewer refugees tend to seek refuge there. This was made clear by the bubble plots in each of the case studies.

Here are the average distances to summarize:

Afghanistan — 383.64 km traveled
Rwanda — 83.30 km traveled
Ukraine — 1172.42 km traveled

However, in our Ukraine case study we also saw a clear difference in how far refugees were willing (or able) to travel. Of course, with the method we're using to calculate distances, the size of the origin country plays a big role. But Afghanistan has a larger land area than Ukraine, and yet the average distance traveled by Ukrainian refugees was three times that of the Afghans. We're starting to form a hypothesis that something has changed over the years, so let's investigate:

We notice three distinct "steps" in this plot:

  • 1962 – ~1973: refugees and asylum seekers would travel between 500 and 900 km to seek safety.
  • 1973 – 1989: we observe a progressive increase in distance travelled, from around 700 km to nearly 4,000 km.
  • 1989 – present: the average per year has stagnated, varying slightly between 4,000 km and 4,500 km.

What could explain this jump between 1973 and 1989? We initially suspected it to be correlated to the sheer increase of displacement data, but we think we can rule that out after adding the total displacement to the graph. There seems to be no obvious correlation between an increase in displacement data and distance traveled. But then, what could it be?

Revolution of commercial flights in the 70s

Back in the 50's and 60's commercial long-haul flights were not yet widely available, and international travel was essentially for business travelers and the wealthy percentage of society. "A coast-to-coast round trip cost around $260, about half of the price of a new automobile," says The National Air and Space Museum. "Yet, new carriers emerged, and new technology began to revolutionize civil aviation."

Long-haul flights became more accessible from 1969 when a new wide-body "jumbo jets" Boeing 747 was introduced on the market. Since the aircraft was much larger than previous models, it significantly increased passenger capacity and lowered costs. What used to be an elite luxury became more affordable for middle class compared to previous decades. "This was not only a milestone in aircraft manufacturing, but also one of the most important events for the development of long-haul air travel. The Boeing 747 gave a much bigger client base access to flying because of its size—it was 2.5 times bigger [...] Over time, lower unit costs for intercontinental trips translated into more affordable air fares, enabling a larger portion of the middle class to fly."
source: Aviation Week — How Boeing's 747 Revolutionized Air Travel

With the explosion of commercial flights from the 70s onwards, we suspect that refugees and asylum seekers may have had more options to seek refuge further away from their home country, and that this may have contributed to the increase in distance traveled observed in our dataset during this period.

In fact, according to this paper from Cross Migration on Migration Infrastructure, it would seem that flights actually tend to offer a safer way out for refugees to leave their country than before. "There are several means of transportation used by migrants including airplanes, coaches and trains, cars and taxis, ships, ferries, boats, rubber dinghies and even bicycles or donkeys. In recent decades, there has been a huge increase in the number of carriers and in particular low budget carriers available to travelers and migrants. For the regular passenger, journeys have become safer, faster, and more affordable, connecting more places than ever before."

In fact, flying became such a popular means of transport for migrants that, in 1987, countries had to introduce the "Carrier Liability Act", which penalizes and fines airlines for transporting passengers without documentation. Over the decades there have been several iterations of this act. It was the origin of the border controls we see today. This journal article, a collaboration between Oxford Law and UNHCR, gives a good overview of the history of this act and its consequences on migration patterns.

Conclusion

In summary

In conclusion, we can say that proximity does seem to be a key factor in the choice of destination for refugees and asylum seekers, but that this has evolved over time. In the earlier decades of our dataset, the tendency for refugees to stay in neighbouring countries was much stronger, while in more recent decades they have been more willing (or able) to travel further away. Since the early 1970s displaced people have had a tendency to travel further as time goes by. The international yearly average plateaus from 1989 at around 4,400 km traveled to seek refuge. We suspect that the revolution of commercial long-haul flights in the 70's may have played a role in this shift, by providing refugees with more options to seek refuge further away from their home country.

However, we should also be careful not to attribute complex social phenomena to single causes. While we are confident that the increased availability of commercial flights is an important contributor, we also suspect that language, culture, political and economic stability and immigration policies of the destination country may also play a role in the choice of destination for refugees and asylum seekers, and that these factors have also evolved over time. These factors are difficult to quantify in terms of hard numerical data, but would be very interesting to investigate in further analysis.
