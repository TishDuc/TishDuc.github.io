Displacements around the globe from 1962-2025

The United Nations Refugee Agency (UNHCR) is the largest global organization providing assistance and protection to forcibly displaced and stateless people. It was founded in 1951 in the aftermath of the second World War, and was originally only intended to provide temporary assistance to European refugees. However, over the years, the UNHCR's mandate has expanded to include people of all nationalities and in all regions of the world. Today, the UNHCR provides assistance to millions of refugees, asylum seekers, internally displaced people, and stateless individuals around the world. The UNHCR's work includes providing emergency shelter, food, water, and medical care to displaced people, as well as advocating for their rights and working to find durable solutions to their displacement.

The UNHCR also publishes data about forcibly displaced people each year. We are going to dive into one of their datasets: the UNHCR Forced Displacement Flow Dataset, which tracks international displacements around the world, from 1962 to 2025. First, we'll analyze the data to identify the biggest refugee crises in the 64 years we have data for, and then we'll dive into three crises from different decades to see whether there might be differences between being a refugee in the 1980s versus 2022.

First, an overview of what the data is, and what it isn't:
Each entry is a 'flow', with an origin country, a destination country, and a number of people.

Pros

  • Covers a long time period (1962-2025)
  • Covers 231 countries
  • Reliable source (UNHCR)

Limitations

  • Only tracks international flows
  • Flows are aggregated year-by-year
  • Many refugees have unknown origin
  • Some displaced people are undocumented
The dataset tracks three kinds of people: refugees, asylum claimants, and people in refugee-like situations. Notably, it does not track internally displaced people, and this is not an insignificant category when talking about forcibly displaced people globally. According to the UNHCR in their 2024 Global Trends Report, internally displaced people accounted for 73.5 million out of the total 123.2 million forcibly displaced people worldwide, approximately 60%, and the percentage has been even higher in previous years.

It's easy to see that the number of displaced people has been increasing for the timeframe, and that they come from increasingly diverse backgrounds. You can see some of the major conflicts of recent history: Afghanistan, Syria, Sudan, Ukraine, Venezuela, Myanmar, etc. Depending on where you live in the world, you may have seen a lot of news coverage about some of them, and others may have flown under your radar. So, instead of looking for crises we already recognize, we'll let the data tell us when and where the crises occurred.

Top 10 displacement episodes from 1962-2025, ranked by total number of people displaced during the episode. Each episode is defined as a continuous period of time with an outgoing flow of displaced people above the median value for that country, with a lower threshold of at least 10,000 people displaced per year.

The graph shows that the top 10 episodes all occurred after 1977, and that the largest episode was the Syrian Civil War, with over 9 million people displaced between 2011 and 2025. Afghanistan appears twice because we detected two separate crisis episodes corresponding roughly to the Soviet-Afghan war of 1979-1989, and the US-led invasion of 2001-2021 (note that displacement flows don't necessarily line up exactly with the conflict periods).

RWANDA 1994-1996:

Short intro of the conflict:
The Rwandan genocide, also known as the Tutsi genocide, occurred from 7 April to 19 July 1994 during the Rwandan Civil War. Over a span of around 100 days, members of the Tutsi ethnic group, as well as some moderate Hutu and Twa, were systematically killed by Hutu militias. Rwandan Constitution states that over 1 million people were killed. The genocide was marked by extreme violence, with victims often murdered by neighbours, and widespread sexual violence.
