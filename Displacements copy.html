Displacements around the globe from 1962-2025

Introduction

The United Nations Refugee Agency (UNHCR) is the largest global organization providing assistance and protection to forcibly displaced and stateless people. It was founded in 1951 in the aftermath of the second World War, and was originally only intended to provide temporary assistance to European refugees. However, over the years, the UNHCR's mandate has expanded to include people of all nationalities and in all regions of the world. Today, the UNHCR provides assistance to millions of refugees, asylum seekers, internally displaced people, and stateless individuals around the world.

The UNHCR also publishes data about forcibly displaced people each year. We are going to dive into one of their datasets: the UNHCR Forced Displacement Flow Dataset, which tracks international displacements around the world, from 1962 to 2025. First, we'll analyze the data to identify the biggest refugee crises in the 64 years we have data for, and then we'll dive into three crises from different decades to see whether there might be differences between being a refugee in the 1980s versus 2022.

First, an overview of what the data is, and what it isn't: each entry is a 'flow', with an origin country, a destination country, and a number of people.

Pros

  • Covers a long time period (1962-2025)
  • Covers 216 countries
  • Reliable source (UNHCR)

Limitations

  • Only tracks international flows
  • Flows are aggregated year-by-year
  • Many refugees have unknown origin
  • Some displaced people are undocumented

The dataset tracks three kinds of people: refugees, asylum claimants, and people in refugee-like situations. Notably, it does not track internally displaced people — according to the UNHCR 2024 Global Trends Report, they accounted for 73.5 million out of 123.2 million forcibly displaced people worldwide, approximately 60%.

Case studies

AFGHANISTAN

The Soviet-Afghan war took place in Afghanistan from Decemper 1979 to February 1989. Though the conflict officially ends in 1989, the instability persisted long afterward and continued after the U.S. invasion in 2001. These decades of sustained instability produced one of the world's largest and most prolonged refugee crises.

source: UNHCR - https://www.unhcr.org/where-we-work/countries/afghanistan

While the conflict lasted from 1979 to 1989, we detected that the period with the most forced displacement was 1980–1988. Looking at the map, three things stand out immediately:

1) The sheer number of people fleeing the country, with some years seeing over 2 million people displaced.
2) The nearby countries of Iran and Pakistan standing out in dark red, accepting the vast majority of Afghan refugees.
3) That the rest of the world seems to be turning a blind eye to the crisis. Other than Pakistan and Iran, very few countries took any refugees at all.

"In fact, Afghanistan has experienced one of the world's largest refugee crises for more than two decades. Between the Soviet invasion of Afghanistan in 1979 and the present day, one in four Afghans has been a refugee. At the peak of the crisis in the late 1980s, there were more than six million Afghan refugees. " source: The Forced Migration Review - https://www.fmreview.org/ruiz/

The soviets started their occupation (between 1980 - 1985) with a "scorched earth campaign", which aimed to cut any support the mujahideen (religious Afghan freedom fighters) might have in the region. This implied the destruction of crops, irrigation systems, and villages and affected local population terribly. By the end of 1981, the widespread destruction of civilian infrastructure further intensified displacement and cross-border asylum movements.

Source: article written by History professor Edward B. Westermann — journals.lib.unb.ca

"Soviet attempts to intimidate the civil population involved a joint air-land effort. The intensive bombardment of villages [...] served as the prelude for the entry of mechanized and armored forces into the area. These forces then proceeded to conduct a 'scorched earth' campaign by destroying the local dwellings, food supplies, crops in the field, irrigation systems, livestock and wells. One Swedish official, after visiting several villages destroyed by the Soviets noted, 'Russian soldiers shot at anything alive in six villages — people, hens, donkeys — and then they plundered what remained of value.' These Soviet operations aimed at driving the villagers out of these areas in an effort to create a cordon sanitaire in which the insurgents would find no support."

But what about the other adjacent countries north of Afghanistan: Turkmenistan, Uzbekistan, and Tajikistan? Why are they marked in grey? Did they not take in any refugees? …Not quite. A Folium map usually uses current country borders, because it relies on modern basemap tiles or modern GeoJSON/shapefiles. Back in 1980, the three aforementioned countries were still part of the USSR bloc and therefore were considered part of the direct attacking force, explaining why no Afghan would seek refuge up north.

Iran from 1.4M in 1981 to 0 in 1982? Why do some countries take in massive numbers one year and then 0 the following year? Policy changes? Did the borders suddenly close? Two things:

  • Status name: Upon closer inspection, it would rather seem that this is more a question of "status" than an actual halt in displacement flows. Though the borders were indeed open during this period, displaced people were not labeled as "refugees" and could therefore be unregistered in our UNHCR dataset. So our data may show 0 while in reality hundreds were still physically crossing into Iran.
  • UNHCR reconstruction of numbers retrospectively: It may have been hard to keep track of the exact flux taking place each year of the conflict, and it could be that some records were incomplete, estimated retrospectively, or merged into a single year. This is something that can occur with older displacement data.

"Until 1992, the Iranian government allowed many Afghans to register as 'involuntary migrants' (mohajerin), gain automatic residency in Iran, and enjoy benefits such as basic healthcare and work permits. Iranian officials effectively treated these registered Afghans as refugees, though that designation was not officially used by the Iranian government."

Source: Human Rights Watch — hrw.org/reports/iran1113_ForUpload_0.pdf

As seen in our preliminary study, we have few data points around this decade. This backs up the above-mentioned point.

Sankey diagram showing the flow of refugees from Afghanistan to different destination countries between 1980 and 1988.

The sankey diagram above confirms just how much of the refugee flow from Afghanistan during this period was concentrated in just two countries, Pakistan and Iran. Pakistan took the most at almost 3.2 million, followed by Iran at nearly 2.5 million, and Germany comes in third with only 21,743.

How far do people go?

Let's have a closer look at the average distance traveled by refugees to flee this conflict.

Bubble chart showing the relationship between distance traveled and number of refugees. Each bubble represents a flow of refugees from Afghanistan to a specific destination country over the conflict period. Bigger bubbles indicate a larger number of refugees. You can hover over each bubble to see the exact distance and number of refugees.

An important disclaimer to the bubble chart is that we don't know exactly how far each refugee actually traveled. Instead, we took the distance from the geographic center of the origin country to the border of the recipient country. This is a rough approximation — it doesn't account for refugees traveling to different parts of the destination country, or taking indirect routes. Still, it gives a general idea of the relationship between distance and number of refugees.

We see the bubbles clustered on the left-hand side of the chart, which means most recipient countries were geographically nearby. We've calculated that Afghan refugees in 1980–1988 traveled an average of 383 kilometers to reach safety. Again, Pakistan and Iran completely dominate. A few European countries also took in some refugees, but the numbers are almost a rounding error compared to the nearly 5.7 million the neighbouring countries received.

Case study:Rwanda

The Rwandan genocide, also known as the Tutsi genocide, occurred from 7 April to 19 July 1994 during the Rwandan Civil War. Over a span of around 100 days, members of the Tutsi ethnic group, as well as some moderate Hutu and Twa, were systematically killed by Hutu militias. An estimated 800,000 Rwandans were killed.

source: BBC — Rwanda genocide: 100 days of slaughter

Note that Rwanda's population only numbered 7 million people in 1994.

source: US Holocaust Memorial Museum — Divided by Ethnicity

The genocide was marked by extreme violence, with victims often murdered by neighbours, and widespread sexual violence. It ended when the Tutsi-led Rwandan Patriotic Front (RPF) won the civil war, but refugees continued to leave the country in the following years. The genocide was followed by the First Congo War in 1996, a conflict between the new RPF government and the Democratic Republic of the Congo.

The map shows a massive exodus of more than 2 million people in 1994, the year the genocide occurred. 1995 was a relatively quiet year, although we still see more than 34,000 people depart to Burundi. Then, when the First Congo War breaks out in 1996, we see another 85,000 people leaving.

Sankey diagram showing the flow of refugees from Rwanda to different destination countries between 1994 and 1996.

As with the Soviet–Afghan war, we see that Rwandan refugees overwhelmingly fled to neighbouring countries. Over the course of the two conflicts, 1.4 million Rwandans fled to the Democratic Republic of the Congo, 700,000 fled to Tanzania, and 294,000 fled to Burundi. A few other countries make the list — most of them African (Uganda, South Africa, Malawi, the Central African Republic), but also Belgium, Germany and France. Of the European countries, Belgium was the biggest recipient, but with only 1,369 Rwandans.

Bubble chart showing the relationship between distance traveled and number of refugees. Each bubble represents a flow of refugees from Rwanda to a specific destination country over the conflict period.

For Rwanda, the picture is much the same as for Afghanistan: the recipient countries overwhelmingly cluster on the left-hand side. Rwandan refugees in this period traveled an average of 83 kilometers to reach safety. However, given the small size of Rwanda and the vast size of the DR Congo and Tanzania, we consider the rough average somewhat less reliable for Rwanda.

Case study:Ukraine

In February 2022, Russia launched a full-scale invasion of Ukraine, leading to the bloodiest conflict in Europe since World War II, and a humanitarian crisis which caused the third-biggest wave of refugees in modern history.

source: Council on Foreign Relations — Ukraine: Conflict at the Crossroads of Europe and Russia

The conflict has resulted in widespread destruction, loss of life, and millions forced to flee. Of the three case studies, this is by far the one that got the most media coverage. The conflict is ongoing, so we don't have the whole story yet, but we can already see some interesting differences in the patterns of displacement compared to the previous two case studies.

Immediately, we see a completely different picture for the Ukraine war than for the previous two conflicts. The map shows that Ukrainians fled to a broad range of countries, including some quite far away. For example, Canada received 75,157 Ukrainian refugees in the first year of the conflict, and Spain received 156,923. Displacements peak in 2022 where millions of Ukrainians flee, but they continue to be high in the following years.

Sankey diagram showing the flow of refugees from Ukraine to different destination countries between 2022 and 2025.

The sankey diagram confirms the differences. For the first time, we see a country that is not a direct neighbour of the conflict (Germany) at number one, receiving 1.4 million Ukrainians. The immediate neighbours of Poland and Russia do receive 1.4 and 1.3 million refugees respectively, but are then followed by Czechia at 640,000, another country that does not border Ukraine. The rest of the list is a mix of European countries, some close (Moldova, Romania, Bulgaria) and some further away (the UK, Spain, Italy).

Bubble chart showing the relationship between distance traveled and number of refugees. Each bubble represents a flow of refugees from Ukraine to a specific destination country over the conflict period.

The bubble chart shows a much more even distribution of recipient countries across the distance spectrum. The average distance traveled by Ukrainian refugees in this period was 1,172 kilometers — more than three times the average distance traveled by Afghan refugees in the 1980s, and more than 14 times the average distance traveled by Rwandan refugees in the 1990s.

Discussion

What we saw so far

After taking a closer look at our case studies, we can see a clear pattern emerging. Not surprisingly, people seem to "favor" proximity in times of crisis. In all three cases the immediate neighbouring countries took in the vast majority of displaced people. On average, the further a country is from the conflict, the fewer refugees tend to seek refuge there.

Afghanistan — 383.64 km traveled
Rwanda — 83.30 km traveled
Ukraine — 1172.42 km traveled

Have the distances traveled changed over time? To get a more general feel, we plotted the yearly average distance traveled across the whole dataset.

The average distance traveled by refugees to their destination country over time. Distance is computed from centroid of origin country to border of destination country.

From our analysis we notice three distinct "steps" in this plot.

• Between 1962 and around 1973: refugees and asylum seekers would travel between 500 and 900 km to seek safety.
• Between 1973 and 1989: we observe a progressive increase in distance travelled, going from around 700 km to nearly 4,000 km.
• From 1989 to present day: the average per year seems to have stagnated and varies slightly from 4,000 km to 4,500 km.

What could explain this increase in distance travelled between 1973 and 1989 in particular? We initially suspected it to be correlated to the sheer increase of data and plotted that as well in the back end. We found no obvious correlation between increase in data and increase in distance traveled, so we can rule out this explanation. Did then means of transportation such as airplanes become more accessible to the public during this 16-year period?

Revolution of commercial flights in the 70s

Back in the 50s and 60s commercial long-haul flights were not yet widely available, and international travel was essentially for business travelers and the wealthy percentage of society. "A coast-to-coast round trip cost around $260, about half of the price of a new automobile," says The National Air and Space Museum. "Yet, new carriers emerged, and new technology began to revolutionize civil aviation."

Long-haul flights became more accessible from 1969 when a new wide-body "jumbo jet" — the Boeing 747 — was introduced on the market. Since the aircraft was much larger than previous models, it significantly increased passenger capacity and lowered costs. What used to be an elite luxury became more affordable for the middle class compared to previous decades.

"This was not only a milestone in aircraft manufacturing, but also one of the most important events for the development of long-haul air travel. The Boeing 747 gave a much bigger client base access to flying because of its size — it was 2.5 times bigger […]. Over time, lower unit costs for intercontinental trips translated into more affordable air fares, enabling a larger portion of the middle class to fly." — Aviation Week

With the explosion of commercial flights from the 70s onwards, we suspect that refugees and asylum seekers may have had more options to seek refuge further away from their home country, and that this may have contributed to the increase in distance traveled observed in our dataset during this period.

In fact, according to a paper from Cross Migration on Migration Infrastructure, it would seem that flights actually tend to offer a safer way out for refugees to leave their country than before.

"There are several means of transportation used by migrants including airplanes, coaches and trains, cars and taxis, ships, ferries, boats, rubber dinghies and even bicycles or donkeys. In recent decades, there has been a huge increase in the number of carriers and in particular low budget carriers available to travellers and migrants. For the regular passenger, journeys have become safer, faster, and more affordable connecting more places than ever before."
