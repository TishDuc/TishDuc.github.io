San Fransisco's crime during
the COVID pandemic and lockdown

Introduction San Francisco has a crime registration database that is publicly accessible and is the center of this analysis. For those who are unfamiliar with this dataset, it contains millions of reported crime incidents that occurred in the city of San Francisco from 2003 to present day. It is still in use today and regularly updated. This list details the time and date of the incident, the district and exact location, category of crime, status of the case, reference number etc. Since the raw data originally has close to 100 different type of crime categories, we've simplifies the task by regrouping some and focusing on the following eight: "assault", "burglary", "weapons", "vandalism", "traffic", "fraud", "drug offenses", "sex offenses". This following analysis is based on our simplified focused crime data. Our first visual overview of the data was a multi-line time series plot showing yearly crime counts of our eight selected “crime categories” from 2003 to 2025. The year 2026 is excluded since, as of the writing of this analysis, it is still an incomplete year. This plot reveals a clear change of crime rate and pattern around the year 2020. This analysis takes a closer look at this shift in the data.

Visualization 1 explanation. Here is the plot in question, though filtered and simplified to only included a selected number of "crime types". We've also and focused it around the time period of interest, mid 2019 to. mid 2021. Looking at the "burglary counts", the orange curve, we observe a clear rise in count from March 2020 onwards, while other crimes like "assult", "fraud", "Drug offense" dip slightlyt but generally remain the same before and after the lockdown of March 2020. Higher Burglary counts? during a lockdown? At first we thought that odd. Why would anyone pick this time to break into people's homes when everyone is forced to be home? Yet Lockdown also means offices, stores, car parks, public spaces are totally empty, and we suspect this explains the rise in burglary counts.
